How to Name Compounds

Six naming systems — each one has a simple pattern. Learn the pattern and you can name anything.

🧱
Binary Ionic Compounds
A metal + one non-metal, held together by opposite charges
metal + non-metal
The Pattern [Metal name unchanged] + [Non-metal root + -ide]
No prefixes. No Roman numerals. Just two words.

3 steps

  1. Write the metal name exactly as shown on the periodic table
  2. Write the non-metal root (drop the element ending)
  3. Add -ide to the non-metal root
Common non-metal roots: fluorine→fluor · chlorine→chlor · bromine→brom · iodine→iod · oxygen→ox · sulfur→sulf · nitrogen→nitr

Examples

NaCl→sodium chloride
MgO→magnesium oxide
Al₂O₃→aluminum oxideno prefixes for ionic!
K₂S→potassium sulfide
BaCl₂→barium chloride
Criss-cross for the formula: the charge of the metal becomes the subscript of the non-metal and vice versa. Reduce if both subscripts match.
⚖️
Multivalent Metals
Some metals have more than one possible charge — you must say which one
Roman numerals
The Pattern [Metal name] (Roman numeral) [non-metal-ide]
The Roman numeral tells you the charge on the metal in that specific compound.

How to find the Roman numeral

  1. Count total negative charge: non-metal subscript × non-metal charge
  2. Divide by the number of metal atoms
  3. That number = Roman numeral. Write it in parentheses after the metal name
Common multivalent metals:
Fe (iron) → 2+ or 3+
Cu (copper) → 1+ or 2+
Co (cobalt) → 2+ or 3+
Pb (lead) → 2+ or 4+
Sn (tin) → 2+ or 4+
Mn (manganese) → 2+, 4+, or 7+

Examples — figuring out the charge

FeCl₂→iron (II) chloride
2 Cl⁻ = 2−, one Fe → Fe = 2+
FeCl₃→iron (III) chloride
3 Cl⁻ = 3−, one Fe → Fe = 3+
Fe₂O₃→iron (III) oxide
3×O²⁻=6−, 2 Fe → each = 3+
PbO₂→lead (IV) oxide
2×O²⁻=4−, one Pb → Pb = 4+
🔗
Polyatomic Ion Compounds
A polyatomic ion is a group of atoms that carries a single charge and never splits apart
multi-atom ions
The Pattern [Metal name] + [Polyatomic ion name as-is]
Never add -ide. The polyatomic ion already has its own name — keep it exactly.

Rules

  1. Write the metal name (if multivalent, add Roman numeral)
  2. Write the polyatomic ion name exactly — never change it to -ide
  3. For formulas: criss-cross charges; put brackets around the ion if its subscript > 1
Ca(OH)₂→calcium hydroxide
Ca²⁺ needs 2 OH⁻ → brackets!
(NH₄)₂CO₃→ammonium carbonate
NH₄⁺ is a cation like a metal
Al(NO₃)₃→aluminum nitrate

Two non-metals sharing electrons — the ratio is fixed, so you must use prefixes
non-metal + non-metal
The Pattern [prefix + first element] + [prefix + second element + -ide]

⚗️
Binary Acids
Hydrogen bonded to a single non-metal, dissolved in water
H + non-metal
The Pattern hydro + [non-metal root] + ic acid
Three parts, always in that order. No spaces inside the name.

Non-metal roots

fluorine→fluor
chlorine→chlor
bromine→brom
iodine→iod
sulfur→sulfur

Examples

HCl (aq)→hydrochloric acid
HBr (aq)→hydrobromic acid
HF (aq)→hydrofluoric acid
HI (aq)→hydroiodic acid
H₂S (aq)→hydrosulfuric acid
H₂ because S is 2−
🌊

Hydrogen bonded to an oxyanion — the acid name mirrors the ion name
H + oxyanion
The Rule — just change the suffix Ion ends in -ate → acid ends in -ic acid
Ion ends in -ite → acid ends in -ous acid
Prefixes per- and hypo- stay exactly the same

Group trends — why these charges
Group 1 — Alkali Metals
Always 1+. They have 1 valence electron and lose it easily to become stable (noble gas config). Na, K, Li, Rb, Cs
Group 2 — Alkaline Earth
Always 2+. They have 2 valence electrons and lose both. Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba
Transition Metals
Variable charges — this is why they're multivalent. Their d-orbitals let them lose different numbers of electrons depending on the reaction.
Group 17 — Halogens
Always 1−. They have 7 valence electrons and gain 1 to reach 8 (full shell). F, Cl, Br, I
Group 16 — Chalcogens
Usually 2−. They have 6 valence electrons and gain 2 to reach 8. O, S, Se, Te
Group 15 — Pnictogens
Usually 3−. They have 5 valence electrons and gain 3. N, P
